The Approach

Empathy + Data + Relational Trust = Movement

Dylan Alejandro and me — on a drive I'll never forget.

The clearest lesson about leadership didn't come from a boardroom or a research lab. It came from a veterinarian's office — when it was time to make an impossible decision about a beloved companion named Dylan Alejandro. His vet didn't tell me what to do. She showed me data — every test result, every trend — and paired it with empathy. With those two things — clarity and care — I was able to make a decision grounded in love, not fear.

That moment mirrors the work. Whether it's partnering with juvenile justice systems in Texas, New Mexico, LA, or D.C., or with child welfare and health care systems in Arkansas, San Diego, or anywhere across the country — the same principles apply:

Empathy + Data + Relational Trust = Movement.

You can't just deliver analysis. As researchers supporting reform, we have to do more. We must build relationships. We need to bring vision, meet systems exactly where they are, and work alongside them to understand what their data is really telling them — and what's possible when they listen.

"Without data, you're just another person with an opinion."

— W. Edwards Deming

But without empathy, data falls flat. And without trust, change stalls. The most powerful work happens when we lead with both heart and evidence.

Empathy.

Without empathy, data falls flat. The best research starts with genuine understanding — of the people inside systems, the communities around them, and the lived experiences that no dataset can fully capture. That's why the insider researchers framework exists: because proximity is a methodology, not just a principle.

Relational Trust.

Without trust, change stalls. Reform doesn't happen through reports delivered from a distance. It happens when researchers and practitioners sit at the same table, meet systems exactly where they are, and build the kind of partnerships where hard truths can be heard — and acted on.

Data.

Without data, you're just another person with an opinion. Data provides the clarity that anchors empathy and trust in evidence — proof that what you're doing is working, that implementation is holding, and that outcomes are real. It turns belief into knowledge.

Throughline: Implementation Science

The methodological discipline that ensures evidence-based practices get embedded in real organizations, sustained through continuous quality improvement, and studied in the conditions where they actually have to work.

Throughline: Process Mining

The technology that reveals what systems are actually doing at scale — not what they think they're doing — making patterns visible that no traditional analysis can see. One methodological. One technological. Both in everything.

Background

Twenty-five years from the courtroom to the research table

Dr. Erin M. Espinosa
PhD in Juvenile Justice · Research Leader · System Reformer

This work started in a Texas juvenile courtroom, where the gap between what systems intended and what young people experienced was impossible to ignore. That experience — as a juvenile probation officer sitting across from families navigating systems that often failed them — became the foundation for everything that followed.

A PhD in Juvenile Justice from Prairie View A&M University provided the research tools. Positions at UT Austin, the University of Maryland, and Evident Change provided the platform to scale. As Director of the Texas Center at the University of Maryland's Institute for Innovation and Implementation, the work expanded to nationwide behavioral health implementation — designing training and technical assistance models anchored in implementation science frameworks, and embedding continuous quality improvement systems to ensure that both the implementation process and the interventions being implemented were actually producing outcomes in real-world practice settings. At Evident Change, a small research unit grew into a department of more than 20 professionals managing a $16+ million portfolio across juvenile justice, child welfare, behavioral health, community violence prevention, education, and social justice research.

Along the way, the work produced the "insider researchers" framework — an approach where individuals with lived experience serve as full research partners. It produced a first-of-its-kind application of commercial process mining to juvenile justice data. And it produced reform partnerships in more than ten states that changed how systems operate for the young people and families they serve.

Now, that full body of experience — the practitioner's empathy, the researcher's rigor, the relational trust built across a national network of reform leaders — is available through independent consulting. Even in the most politically charged and economically uncertain times, this work has partnered with leaders as they shift upstream — keeping kids out of detention, focused on futures, not failure. Because the most powerful work happens when we lead with both heart and evidence.

Dallas County Juvenile Justice Reform

A long-term partnership with Dallas County's 304th and 305th District Courts built on relational trust between researchers and practitioners. The work included developing evidence-based decision matrices, petition and disposition frameworks, and a continuous quality improvement process — all co-designed with the implementation team. Innovation

Process Mining for Juvenile Justice & Mental Health Systems

The first application of commercial process mining technology to juvenile justice data — a partnership with William & Mary School of Business and Celonis that revealed system patterns invisible to traditional methods. By analyzing population-based datasets rather than samples, the work identified "detention spirals" and gaps between clinical needs and treatment received. Presented internationally at Celosphere 2024 and 2025 in Munich, the approach is now generating global interest as a transparency framework for government systems.

Juvenile Justice

NIJ Girls & Gangs — Rio Grande Valley

An NIJ-funded study that put the insider researchers framework into practice — and demonstrated what becomes possible when empathy is treated as methodology. A team of women with lived expertise in gang ecologies conducted 150 in-depth interviews with gang-connected girls — building the trust needed to surface stories that outside researchers would never hear. The work identified "hang around" status — a liminal position affecting nearly half of participants — and revealed that gang labels increase penalties for boys but have no effect on girls' judicial outcomes, challenging fundamental assumptions in the field. The empathy opened the door. The data changed the conversation.

Implementation

Nationwide Behavioral Health Implementation

As Director of the Texas Center at the University of Maryland's Institute for Innovation and Implementation, the challenge was bridging the gap between evidence-based behavioral health practices and the real-world conditions where they needed to work. The response was an implementation science approach that treated training, technical assistance, and continuous quality improvement as integrated — not sequential — components. Implementation frameworks guided how practices were introduced. CQI systems monitored whether those practices were being delivered with fidelity. And outcomes studies assessed whether they were actually producing results in the complex, resource-constrained environments where practitioners operate every day. The model demonstrated that you can't separate the question of "does it work?" from "is it being implemented well enough to work?"

Child Welfare

QIC-H2: Community Alternatives to CPS Reporting

A Quality Improvement Center partnership with the Children's Bureau examining whether communities can support families without relying solely on investigation-driven systems. The work includes a 50-state neglect statutory review and synthesis of helpline and hotline reform approaches — contributing to the national conversation about what child protection looks like when it leads with support rather than surveillance.

Violence Prevention

California Violence Intervention & Prevention

Multi-site evaluation of CalVIP-funded community violence prevention programs across the state. Cross-site analysis supports state-level evidence building for community-driven intervention strategies — helping California understand what works, where, and for whom.
